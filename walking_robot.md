## Walking Robot Design Project (January 2025 - April 2025)
Mechanisms and Vibrations (AER 403): Toronto Metropolitan University

### Objective
Design, analyze, and fabricate a mechanically efficient walking robot using linkage‑based locomotion, with emphasis on kinematic feasibility, dynamic stability, vibration behavior, and manufacturability. The objective was to develop a single‑motor, single‑degree‑of‑freedom walking mechanism capable of traversing a three‑meter course under competition constraints.

### Engineering Tools and Methods  
**Engineering Tools**  
- **MATLAB:** kinematic modeling, displacement and velocity analysis, motion simulation, trade‑study evaluation  
- **SolidWorks:** 3D CAD modeling, mechanism assembly, constraint validation, manufacturability assessment  
- **OnShape:** parametric CAD modeling, linkage layout refinement, collaborative design iteration  
- **Laser Cutting:** fabrication of lightweight wooden structural components for rapid prototyping  

**Engineering Methods**
- **Kinematic Analysis:** Evaluated position, velocity, and motion profiles of linkage‑based locomotion mechanisms.  
- **Mechanism Synthesis:** Designed and compared four‑bar, slider‑crank, and multi‑link creeping mechanisms to identify optimal locomotion strategies.  
- **Degrees‑of‑Freedom (DOF) Analysis:** Assessed and selected single‑DOF mechanisms to maximize mechanical efficiency and control simplicity.  
- **Engineering Trade Studies:** Analyzed gait stability versus system complexity, torque requirements versus motion smoothness, and performance versus manufacturability.  
- **Motion Simulation & Visualization:** Used animation and simulation to evaluate gait behavior, ground contact consistency, and overall motion feasibility.  
- **Linkage‑Based Mechanical Design:** Designed mechanically driven locomotion systems with attention to load paths and motion translation.  
- **Dynamic Stability & Vibration Considerations:** Incorporated stability and vibration effects into mechanism selection and refinement.  
- **Design for Manufacturability (DFM):** Applied DFM principles to ensure feasible fabrication and assembly.  
- **Prototype Fabrication & Assembly:** Built and assembled physical prototypes for validation.  
- **Experimental Validation & Performance Testing:** Verified simulated behavior through physical testing.  
- **Systems Engineering Methods:** Performed concept evaluation and down‑selection using simulation results, followed by iterative design refinement.  
- **Team‑Based Engineering & Verification:** Collaborated in a team environment and verified performance against defined system and competition constraints.  

### Technical Contributions  
- Led the conceptual design, analysis, and manufacturing of a walking robot (Creeper) for the AER403 Walking Robot Competition, executing the full development cycle from concept selection through prototype testing.  
- Evaluated multiple candidate locomotion mechanisms, including:  
  - Four‑bar linkage  
  - Slider‑crank mechanism  
  - Multi‑link creeping mechanism  
- Developed MATLAB‑based kinematic models for each candidate to analyze:  
  - Link displacement and velocity profiles  
  - Gait consistency and ground contact behavior  
  - Mechanical efficiency and feasibility  
- Generated motion animations and quantitative performance metrics to support design trade‑off analysis between stability, torque requirements, and system complexity.  
- Selected the Creeper mechanism based on superior kinematic smoothness, single‑degree‑of‑freedom motion, reduced torque demand, and improved ground contact stability.  
- Created detailed CAD models using SolidWorks and OnShape, validating constraints, linkage motion, and manufacturability prior to fabrication.  
- Fabricated a lightweight prototype using laser‑cut wooden components, integrating mechanical joints and drive transmission with a single motor input.  

### Results & Outcomes  
- Delivered a fully functional walking robot prototype capable of stable, repeatable locomotion across the competition course using a single motor and linkage‑based mechanism.  
- Achieved smooth and mechanically efficient conversion of rotational input into forward translational motion through a single‑DOF linkage system.  
- Demonstrated improved gait stability and motion consistency compared to alternative mechanisms evaluated during concept selection.  
- Validated kinematic predictions through experimental testing, confirming realistic motion behavior and controlled vibration response.  
- Successfully met performance, stability, and manufacturability requirements under competition constraints.  
- Strengthened applied understanding of mechanisms design, kinematics, vibration principles, and engineering trade‑off analysis consistent with real‑world mechanical product development.
